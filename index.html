ME/CFS Management

Stabilization Protocol

A layered approach to reducing persistent danger signaling, supporting mitochondrial energy production, and improving autonomic stability in ME/CFS and Long COVID.

I have lived with ME/CFS for 35+ years. During that time I have recovered from a very severe baseline three times — each a prolonged period of severely reduced functioning, not a temporary crash. Three of my children also developed severe ME/CFS and have fully recovered and no longer have the illness. This protocol reflects what I have learned through that personal experience and through ongoing independent research. It describes the personal approach I have used for myself and my children — it is not a generalized treatment program. I am simply sharing what has worked for us.
Work in progress: This protocol is actively being refined as my research and experience continue. Additional detail, citations, and sections will be added over time. What you see here represents the current state of my thinking, not a finished or final document.

Layer 1 of 3

Foundational Layer — Baseline Support

Establish conditions that allow recovery. This layer must come first. Without it, other interventions are unlikely to produce lasting improvement.

Key principles
  • Strict pacing — stay within your energy envelope at all times
  • Consistent sleep and wake timing every day
  • Daily rest period or nap (non-negotiable)
  • Minimize autonomic spikes (sudden exertion, temperature shifts, stress)
  • Avoid PEM — going one year without PEM can significantly support recovery
  • Hydration: 2–3 litres of filtered water daily
  • Include electrolytes; add salt if you experience orthostatic symptoms
  • Prioritize protein at each meal; include healthy fats
  • Keep carbohydrates lower and stable to prevent blood sugar swings
  • Take a daily multivitamin
Tools
  • Pacing app (e.g. Visible) with heart rate monitor
  • Wearable for heart rate, stress, Body Battery, and HRV monitoring (e.g. Garmin)
  • Consistent daily rhythm — same structure each day
  • Optional: practical support through hired help and/or caregivers, if available
Key principles
  • Reduce sensory overwhelm — noise, bright light, visual clutter
  • Maintain predictable routines and environments
  • Simplify surroundings to reduce cognitive load
  • Create calm, low-demand spaces for rest and recovery
Practices
  • Structured home environment — order reduces cognitive cost
  • Use calming sensory supports: soft lighting, controlled sound, comfortable temperature
  • Limit exposure to chaotic or overstimulating environments where possible

Emotional safety directly influences nervous system regulation. Reducing internal threat signals supports all other protocol layers.

Key principles
  • Practice joy — small, genuine moments of enjoyment daily
  • Allow authentic self-expression; reduce masking where possible
  • Connect regularly with safe, loving people and/or animals
  • Gently process and release trauma over time
Practices
  • Intentional moments of play, creativity, or lightness
  • Relational connection: family, trusted friends, pets
  • Emotional processing: journaling, therapy, somatic work, EMDR, reflection
  • Environments and activities that feel safe and regulating

How the foundation supports ME/CFS stability

→Physical load reduction — lowers physiological strain signals from overexertion, dehydration, blood sugar swings
→Sensory load reduction — reduces autonomic spikes and external stress triggers
→Internal safety signaling — reduces persistent threat interpretation in the nervous system

Together: lower baseline inflammation → more stable autonomic regulation → reduced mitochondrial stress signaling → fewer crashes, more predictable daily function.

Layer 2 of 3

Regulatory Layer — System Regulation

Reduce upstream signaling that keeps the body in a defensive "threat-on" state. Only effective when the foundation layer is in place.

Nicotine patch (low dose)
Possible mechanisms
  • Stimulates alpha-7 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors
  • Activates the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway
  • Suppresses excess cytokine signaling
  • May reduce microglial activation
Typical patient approach
  • Low-dose patch, worn continuously with periodic breaks
  • Discontinue if not well-tolerated
Note: Discuss with your physician before starting. This is an off-label use.
Children & adolescents: Nicotine is not appropriate for use in children or adolescents under any circumstances. Do not use without explicit direction from a pediatric specialist.
Hypnosis / deep relaxation audio
Purpose
  • Reinforce parasympathetic dominance
  • Retrain brain threat-prediction networks
Likely mechanisms
  • Reduced amygdala threat activation
  • Increased vagal tone
  • Decreased cortisol and sympathetic drive
Daily use over weeks may gradually retrain the nervous system toward a stable baseline state.
Low Dose Naltrexone (LDN)
Proposed mechanisms
  • Transient opioid receptor blockade → rebound endorphin release
  • Suppression of microglial activation
  • Reduced pro-inflammatory cytokines
Potential benefits
  • Reduced neuroinflammation
  • Improved sleep quality
  • Reduced pain
  • Improved energy stability
Note: LDN requires a prescription. Re-establishment after a pause takes 4–6 weeks; subjective impression during that window is not a reliable signal of effectiveness.
Children & adolescents: LDN use in children requires pediatric medical supervision and a prescription. Do not administer to a child without explicit guidance from their physician.
Practical tip: The simplest way to incorporate the adaptogens, mushroom powder, and kefir together is a daily smoothie. Use powdered forms of the adaptogens and mushrooms, blend with kefir and berries. This improves absorption and makes consistency much easier.
Children & adolescents: Adaptogens, medicinal mushroom powders, and other supplements in this section have not been adequately studied in children. Do not use these with children or adolescents without first consulting their pediatrician or a qualified healthcare provider.
Eleuthero (Siberian ginseng)
  • HPA-axis regulation
  • Improved stress tolerance
  • Mild mitochondrial support
Astragalus
  • Immune modulation
  • Antiviral signaling support
  • Mitochondrial protection
Medicinal mushroom powder

Contains beta-glucans and polysaccharides.

  • Immune balancing
  • Microglial regulation
  • Antioxidant activity
Kefir (gut + immune support)
  • Supports gut microbiome through probiotics
  • Improves gut barrier function and digestion
  • Influences immune signaling via the gut-immune axis
Combining kefir with adaptogen powders (e.g. in a smoothie with berries) appears more effective than adaptogens alone — likely improves absorption and overall system response.
Layer 3 of 3

Support Layer — Capacity & Recovery

Support energy production, reduce secondary stress signals, and assist recovery. Only effective when layers 1 and 2 are in place.

Children & adolescents: CoQ10, ALCAR, and NADH have limited safety data in children. Do not use these supplements with children or adolescents without consulting their pediatrician.
CoQ10
  • Core component of the mitochondrial electron transport chain
  • Supports ATP generation; reduces oxidative stress
Acetyl-L-Carnitine (ALCAR)
  • Transports fatty acids into mitochondria for energy production
  • Supports acetylcholine production
  • May improve cognitive clarity and reduce fatigue
NADH
  • Directly feeds electrons into mitochondrial complex I
  • Supports cellular ATP generation upstream of CoQ10
Synergy: NADH → CoQ10 → ALCAR support three different stages of mitochondrial energy metabolism. Together they address both fuel delivery and ATP production.

These three are taken together to support glutathione production and recycling — a key cellular antioxidant system.

Children & adolescents: NAC, high-dose glycine, and vitamin C protocols have not been established as safe for children at adult doses. Consult a pediatrician before use in children or adolescents.
NAC (N-acetylcysteine)
  • Provides cysteine — a required building block for glutathione
  • Supports antioxidant defense systems
Glycine
  • Second required building block for glutathione synthesis
  • Supports nervous system calming and sleep quality
  • 3g at bedtime leverages dual role: glutathione precursor + sleep support
Vitamin C
  • Recycles oxidized glutathione back to its active form
  • Cofactor for carnitine synthesis — links to mitochondrial fuel delivery
  • Supports mitochondrial membrane integrity
  • Sodium ascorbate form adds sodium, supporting POTS/orthostatic symptoms
Dosing notes
  • Distribute across the day in 1–2g increments; do not take a single large bolus
  • Titrate upward slowly; reduce by 1–2g if loose stools occur
  • Sodium ascorbate and liposomal forms are better tolerated than ascorbic acid, especially without food
  • Separate from medications by 30–60 minutes
Alpha Lipoic Acid (ALA) — optional, physician review required
  • Cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase — fills a gap not addressed by CoQ10, NADH, or ALCAR
  • Recycles glutathione, vitamin C, and vitamin E back to active forms
  • Crosses the blood-brain barrier; reduces microglial activation
Caution: ALA may affect thyroid hormone metabolism (T3/T4 conversion). Physician review is required before trialing if you have a thyroid condition or take thyroid-related medications.
Lymphatic support
  • Lymphatic rebounding (gentle, short duration)
  • Dry brushing (light pressure, brief)
Consistency matters more than intensity. Stop before fatigue or symptom escalation.
Gentle detox support
  • Herbal teas that support liver function: dandelion, milk thistle, ginger, peppermint
  • Aim for regular, low-effort consistency — not aggressive protocols

Many people with ME/CFS notice a seasonal pattern — better in summer, worse in winter. Vitamin D levels, circadian rhythm disruption, thermoregulation cost, and viral reactivation risk all vary with season and may contribute to this cycle.

Vitamin D3 + K2 (MK-7)
Why it matters
  • Vitamin D3 modulates T cell activity and reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines
  • Deficiency worsens in winter at northern latitudes — supplementation needs to compensate
  • K2 (MK-7 form) is a required cofactor — directs calcium to bones and away from arteries
  • Magnesium is required for enzymatic activation of vitamin D — ensure adequate magnesium intake
Practical guidance
  • Daily dosing maintains more stable serum levels than the same total dose taken 2–3 times per week
  • Take with a fat-containing meal — vitamin D is fat-soluble
  • Supplement year-round, not only in dark months
  • Target serum level: 50–80 ng/mL (test via 25-hydroxy vitamin D blood test)
  • K2 dose: 100–200 mcg/day as MK-7 form — discuss with physician
Morning light therapy
  • 10,000 lux lamp within 1 hour of waking — stabilizes melatonin/cortisol rhythm
  • Start at 10 minutes and titrate up to 20–30 minutes as tolerated
  • Does not require looking directly at lamp — can be used during breakfast or rest
  • Stop if symptoms worsen — photosensitivity is a documented ME/CFS feature
Thermal load reduction
  • Thermoregulation in cold is a real metabolic energy cost that does not appear in pacing metrics
  • Heated blanket during rest eliminates the thermogenic cost of warming from cold
  • Layer clothing (especially extremities) rather than relying on room heating alone
Pre-emptive pacing reduction — start October
  • The November crash depletion curve begins during the high-function summer/fall phase
  • Reduce your pacing ceiling proactively from October 1 — regardless of how well you feel
  • Treat October as a mandatory transition buffer month, not an extension of summer
Holiday load pre-planning — November through January
  • The holiday period concentrates sensory, social, and logistical demands at the biological nadir of the year
  • Pre-negotiate reduced commitments before the season begins — not reactively
  • Delegate household and logistics tasks in advance of key events
  • Schedule mandatory recovery days around high-load events before they happen

Working Hypothesis — The Danger Response Model

Many ME/CFS and Long COVID researchers propose that the body becomes stuck in a persistent danger response state. This protocol is designed to interrupt that state at multiple points simultaneously.

Key research: Liu Z, Hollmann C, Kalanidhi S, et al. Immunoglobulin G complexes from post-infectious ME/CFS, including post-COVID ME/CFS disrupt cellular energetics and alter inflammatory marker secretion. Brain Behav Immun Health. 2026 Feb 4;52:101187. doi: 10.1016/j.bbih.2026.101187. PMID: 41704659.

Key features of the danger response state

  • Immune cells remain partially activated
  • Microglia in the brain stay sensitized
  • The autonomic nervous system favors sympathetic "alarm" signaling
  • Mitochondria shift toward defensive metabolism rather than efficient ATP production
  • Energy production becomes inefficient
Result: fatigue, PEM, autonomic instability, cognitive fog, non-restorative sleep.

Why the sequence matters

Layer 1 Foundation — removes ongoing stress inputs first
Layer 2 Regulatory — reduces upstream danger signaling (nervous system + immune)
Layer 3 Support — improves cellular function and recovery capacity

This order ensures stability is established before capacity is increased, and that signaling is regulated before metabolic demand is raised.
